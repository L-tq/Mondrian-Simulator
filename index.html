The Art of De Stijl

Piet Mondrian was a Dutch painter and a pioneer of abstract art. His iconic compositions reduce visual language to its purest elements: horizontal and vertical black lines intersecting on a white canvas, punctuated by primary colors — red, blue, and yellow.

This simulator recreates that spirit algorithmically, using recursive subdivision and visual weight balancing to generate unique Mondrian-style compositions at the click of a button.

How It Works

01

Split

The algorithm starts with a single rectangle and recursively subdivides it. Lines are placed with a bias toward aesthetically pleasing proportions like the golden ratio and 2:3.

02

Color

Colors are assigned strategically — red to the largest rectangles, blue and yellow to edge-adjacent spaces, and a rare black accent for contrast. Most rectangles remain white.

03

Balance

Colors are swapped iteratively to pull the visual center of mass toward the geometric center, creating the harmonious tension characteristic of Mondrian's work.

Press R/B/Y/W/K to recolor, H/V to split, M to merge, Del to clear, and Escape to deselect. Click Generate or use the step-by-step animation to create new compositions.

How to Use the Simulator

1

Generate a Composition

Click the Generate button to create a new Mondrian-style composition instantly, or use the step-by-step animation to watch the algorithm build it piece by piece.

2

Adjust the Settings

Open the settings panel to tweak the Grid size (12–200 cells), Rect count (5–40), Line Gap, Line Width, T-Junction rate, and Proportional Bias to get the exact look you want.

3

Click to Edit Regions

Click any colored rectangle to select it, then pick a new color from the toolbar or press R B Y W K to recolor.

4

Split and Merge

Press H to split a region horizontally or V to split vertically. Hold Shift and click two regions, then press M to merge them together.

5

Toggle Line Styles

Click a line near the canvas edge to create edge gaps, or select a line and press T to switch between regular and thick line styles for added visual weight.
